EP Legislative Procedures: Ukraine, Defence & Sovereignty — March 2026

The European Parliament's January–February 2026 plenary sessions produced 56 adopted texts spanning Ukraine support loans, strategic defence partnerships, technological sovereignty, climate neutrality, and migration reform — while fresh 2026 legislative procedures accelerate across all policy domains.

Between 20 January and 24 February 2026, the European Parliament adopted 56 texts in the TA-10-2026 series, marking one of the most intensive legislative periods of the 10th parliamentary term. The January plenary alone delivered breakthrough texts on pharmaceutical security (TA-10-2026-0001), a new legal framework for innovative companies — the "28th Regime" (TA-10-2026-0002) — and enhanced cooperation on a Loan for Ukraine (TA-10-2026-0010). February added critical measures amending the Ukraine Facility (TA-10-2026-0036), the multiannual financial framework (TA-10-2026-0037), and EU strategic defence partnerships (TA-10-2026-0040). As new 2026 procedures — including 2026/0008(COD) through 2026/0059(COD) — enter the pipeline, Europe's legislative machinery is operating at full capacity.

New 2026 Legislative Procedures

The 2026 legislative cycle has opened with a significant wave of new procedures under the Ordinary Legislative Procedure (COD). Registered filings include 2026/0008(COD), 2026/0010(COD), 2026/0011(COD), 2026/0012(COD), and 2026/0013(COD), all now entering committee stage, with the more recent 2026/0044(COD), 2026/0045(COD), and 2026/0059(COD) freshly filed. These cover a broad spectrum from regulatory simplification to industrial and digital policy.

Budget procedures are equally active: 2026/0001(BUD), 2026/0004(BUD), 2026/0037(BUD), and 2026/0038(BUD) reflect Parliament's early engagement with financing instruments, including the mobilisation of emergency funds. Non-legislative procedures (NLE) include 2026/0041(NLE) and 2026/0058(NLE), covering institutional appointments and international agreements.

Own-initiative procedures (INI) numbered 2026/2003 through 2026/2015 are advancing across committees, addressing topics such as democratic oversight, foreign policy, and internal market reform. Regulatory (RSP), delegated act (DEA), and immunity (IMM) procedures — including 2026/2518 and subsequent filings — further demonstrate the Parliament's cross-institutional workload in early 2026.

January–February 2026: Key Adopted Texts

The January 2026 plenary (20–22 January, Strasbourg) delivered landmark legislation. Parliament adopted the framework for critical medicinal products security (TA-10-2026-0001), directly addressing Europe's pharmaceutical supply vulnerabilities exposed during the pandemic era. The "28th Regime" (TA-10-2026-0002) creates an optional supranational legal framework for innovative companies seeking pan-European incorporation, a long-demanded reform by startups and scale-ups. A Just Transition Directive (TA-10-2026-0003) extends workers' rights protections across green economy transitions.

January's geopolitical texts proved equally consequential. Parliament approved enhanced cooperation on the Ukraine Support Loan (TA-10-2026-0010), establishing financing arrangements for Ukraine's reconstruction and defence. The EU–Bosnia and Herzegovina operational agreement with the European Border and Coast Guard Agency (TA-10-2026-0011) strengthened external border management, while annual reports on the Common Foreign and Security Policy (TA-10-2026-0012) and Common Security and Defence Policy (TA-10-2026-0013) set the strategic framework for 2026. Parliament also adopted its position on air passenger rights (TA-10-2026-0009) and the European Electoral Act reform (TA-10-2026-0006).

The January 22 sitting produced the Drones and new systems of warfare resolution (TA-10-2026-0020), calling on the EU to adapt its regulatory and procurement frameworks for autonomous military systems, alongside a text on European technological sovereignty and digital infrastructure (TA-10-2026-0022) — a strategic companion to the EU's AI Act implementation.

February 2026 (10–12 February and 24 February, Strasbourg) delivered the most consequential financial package. Parliament voted to amend the Ukraine Facility regulation (TA-10-2026-0036), extended the Ukraine Support Loan for 2026–2027 (TA-10-2026-0035), and amended the Multiannual Financial Framework 2021–2027 (TA-10-2026-0037) to accommodate increased Ukraine support and defence investments. The adoption of EU Strategic Defence and Security Partnerships (TA-10-2026-0040) marked a qualitative shift in Europe's collective security posture. On migration, Parliament adopted a revised safe countries of origin list (TA-10-2026-0025) and updated the safe third country concept (TA-10-2026-0026), accelerating the operationalisation of the New Pact on Migration and Asylum. The February 24 resolution on four years of Russia's war of aggression against Ukraine (TA-10-2026-0056) reaffirmed Parliament's unwavering political support.

Strategic Context

The January–February 2026 legislative output reflects a Parliament operating under acute geopolitical pressure. Russia's ongoing war in Ukraine — now in its fourth year — has driven a cluster of financial, security, and defence texts that would have been unthinkable in the pre-2022 EU. The MFF amendment (TA-10-2026-0037) and Ukraine Facility amendment (TA-10-2026-0036) together represent a substantial reallocation of EU budget resources toward external security priorities.

The technological sovereignty agenda (TA-10-2026-0022), paired with the drones and autonomous warfare resolution (TA-10-2026-0020), signals a Parliament increasingly focused on Europe's strategic autonomy in both civilian and military technology. The "28th Regime" (TA-10-2026-0002) for innovative companies addresses the innovation competitiveness gap identified in the Draghi report, offering companies an EU-wide legal identity without requiring full legislative harmonisation across 27 member states.

On migration, the simultaneous adoption of the safe countries of origin list (TA-10-2026-0025) and safe third country concept update (TA-10-2026-0026) demonstrates Parliament's commitment to operationalising the New Pact on Migration and Asylum, though these texts remain politically contested between liberal and conservative groups.

Stakeholder Impact

Pharmaceutical and healthcare industries gain legal certainty through the critical medicines framework (TA-10-2026-0001), which introduces EU-level stockpiling obligations and supply chain monitoring. Technology companies and startups welcome the "28th Regime" (TA-10-2026-0002) as a genuine alternative to national incorporation in fragmented European legal environments. Airlines and passengers gain strengthened rights under TA-10-2026-0009. Workers in transition industries benefit from the Just Transition Directive (TA-10-2026-0003). Ukraine's government and people receive continued financing certainty through the extended loan and facility mechanisms. EU member states and defence industries gain a clearer strategic framework through the defence partnerships resolution (TA-10-2026-0040).

Why This Matters

The 56 texts adopted in just six weeks of 2026 plenaries reflect a European Parliament that has found its legislative stride in the 10th term. Taken together, these measures address Europe's most urgent vulnerabilities: the dependence on external pharmaceutical supply chains, the technological gap with US and Chinese rivals, the security challenge posed by Russia's aggression, and the societal pressures of migration and just transition. For citizens, businesses, and member states, the 2026 legislative cycle is already defining the regulatory landscape for the rest of the decade. Tracking the new 2026/XXXX procedures as they move through committee will be essential for anticipating what comes next.
