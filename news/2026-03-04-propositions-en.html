Legislative Proposals: European Parliament Monitor

Recent legislative proposals, procedure tracking, and pipeline status in the European Parliament

The European Parliament's tenth legislative term is entering its second year with a busy pipeline of proposals, as EP10 ramps up legislative activity following the June 2024 elections. With 720 MEPs now fully settled into their committee roles and a rightward political shift shaping priorities, the current legislative session is defined by a wave of ordinary legislative procedures (COD), non-legislative files (NLE), and budgetary instruments (BUD) that reflect the EU's evolving agenda on defence, digital sovereignty, trade, and institutional reform.

Recent Legislative Proposals

The Parliament is processing at least 20 new procedures filed in 2025 alone. The majority use the Ordinary Legislative Procedure (COD), confirming the European Parliament's co-legislative authority on the most consequential files. Key procedure references include:

  • 2025/0012(COD) — An ordinary legislative file reflecting co-decision by Parliament and Council on a regulatory measure currently progressing through committee referral.
  • 2025/0021(COD), 2025/0022(COD), 2025/0023(COD) — A cluster of three COD procedures introduced in rapid succession, signalling a coordinated legislative push across multiple policy domains.
  • 2025/0039(COD) through 2025/0059(COD) — A further wave of ordinary legislative proposals, with procedure numbers advancing quickly, indicating a high legislative intake rate for EP10's second year.
  • 2025/0009(NLE), 2025/0009M(NLE) — Non-legislative files, likely relating to international agreements or institutional appointments requiring Parliament's consent.
  • 2025/0046(NLE), 2025/0055(NLE), 2025/0066(NLE) — Additional NLE procedures covering consent-based dossiers.
  • 2025/0061(BUD) — A budgetary procedure, part of the EU's annual budget cycle now under Parliament's scrutiny.

This distribution — with COD procedures outnumbering NLE and BUD files by roughly three to one — is consistent with historical patterns observed across EP6 through EP9, where ordinary legislative procedures have comprised the largest share of Parliament's workload.

Adopted Texts: What Parliament Has Decided

In addition to new proposals entering the pipeline, the Parliament has adopted significant texts in early 2025 that shape the legislative landscape:

  • VAT: Rules for the Digital Age (TA-10-2025-0012, adopted 12 February 2025) — A landmark reform modernising VAT collection for digital platforms and cross-border e-commerce, directly affecting millions of businesses across the single market.
  • Administrative Cooperation in Taxation (TA-10-2025-0013, adopted 12 February 2025) — Strengthening information-sharing between member state tax authorities to combat tax avoidance.
  • Common Data Platform on Chemicals (TA-10-2025-0045, adopted 1 April 2025) — Establishing a monitoring and outlook framework for chemicals, a key pillar of the EU's zero-pollution ambition.
  • Reform and Growth Facility for Moldova (TA-10-2025-0022, adopted 11 March 2025) — A strategic geopolitical instrument supporting Moldova's reform agenda and EU approximation path.
  • Macro-Financial Assistance to Jordan (TA-10-2025-0048, adopted 1 April 2025) — Extending financial support to a key neighbourhood partner.
  • Corporate Sustainability Reporting Amendments (TA-10-2025-0064, adopted 3 April 2025) — Amending the timelines for sustainability reporting and due diligence requirements under Directives 2022/2464 and 2024/1760, a high-profile file affecting corporate compliance across the EU.
  • Common Security and Defence Policy (TA-10-2025-0058, adopted 2 April 2025) — The annual CSDP implementation report for 2024, reflecting heightened defence cooperation in the wake of geopolitical tensions.

Legislative Pipeline Overview

The pipeline health score currently registers at 100 with strong legislative momentum, according to the EP's open data. All ten sampled active procedures show low bottleneck risk, with no stalled files detected. The throughput rate confirms a steady flow of proposals through committee stages toward plenary consideration.

Historically, EP10 is following the characteristic curve: the first year (2024) saw reduced output during the post-election transition, with 56 legislative acts adopted — roughly 40% below the EP9 peak of 148 acts in 2023. Now, in 2026, predictions suggest output will climb toward 102 acts as committees reach full operational capacity. By 2027, legislative output is forecast at 125 acts, approaching the mid-term peak that has characterised every parliamentary term since EP6.

The parliamentary fragmentation index stands at 6.59 — the highest in EU history — meaning no two-party coalition can command a majority. The effective number of parties has risen from 4.12 in 2004 to 6.59 in 2026. This structural shift means every major legislative file now requires negotiation across at least three political groups, increasing the time and complexity of coalition-building in committee and plenary.

Strategic Context: EP10's Evolving Priorities

The composition of the current pipeline reveals the EU's shifting legislative priorities under EP10. The rightward political rebalancing — with the Eurosceptic and far-right bloc's seat share reaching 15.6% and EPP consolidating leadership — has implications for the types of proposals that advance quickly versus those that stall.

Defence and security files (including the CSDP annual report) are advancing with broad cross-party consensus. Digital regulation, following EP9's landmark Digital Services Act and AI Act, continues through implementation-phase dossiers. Meanwhile, sustainability reporting timelines have already been amended, reflecting industry pressure to recalibrate compliance deadlines.

The top-two group concentration (CR₂) has fallen to 44.5%, confirming that the traditional EPP-S&D grand coalition — which commanded automatic majorities from 2004 to 2019 — can no longer pass legislation alone. The minimum winning coalition now requires three groups, a structural change that empowers centrist blocs like Renew Europe and elevates the Green and ECR groups as potential kingmakers on specific files.

Stakeholder Impact

Businesses: The VAT digital age reform and corporate sustainability reporting amendments directly affect compliance costs and operational frameworks for EU and international companies trading in the single market.

Member States: Enhanced tax cooperation and the budgetary procedure underline fiscal coordination pressures, while the Moldova facility signals enlargement-adjacent financial commitments.

Civil Society: The chemicals data platform and sustainability directives create new transparency tools for environmental and consumer advocacy organisations.

EU Neighbours: Macro-financial assistance packages and reform facilities for Moldova and Jordan demonstrate the Parliament's role in shaping the EU's geopolitical footprint through legislative instruments.

Why This Matters

The volume and character of proposals entering Parliament's pipeline in early 2026 signal a legislative term that is accelerating toward its productive mid-cycle. With fragmentation at record levels, legislative outcomes will be determined not by traditional bloc politics but by ad hoc coalitions formed file by file. For citizens, businesses, and member state governments, understanding this pipeline is essential: the proposals being processed today will shape European regulation on taxation, chemicals, defence, sustainability, and neighbourhood policy for years to come. Tracking these procedures from introduction through committee to plenary vote provides the earliest possible signal of regulatory change.
