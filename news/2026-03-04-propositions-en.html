Legislative Procedures: European Parliament Monitor

Recent legislative proposals, procedure tracking, and pipeline status in the European Parliament

The European Parliament has registered over 90 new legislative procedures in 2025 alone, with the latest batch — including 2025/0232(COD) through 2025/0241(COD) — now entering committee stage under the Ordinary Legislative Procedure. Meanwhile, seven recently updated adopted texts (TA-10-2025-0281 through TA-10-2025-0308) signal continued momentum on files ranging from corporate sustainability to chemicals regulation. As the 10th parliamentary term moves into its second full year, the legislative pipeline reflects the institution's shifting priorities toward defence spending, industrial competitiveness, and regulatory simplification.

Recent Legislative Proposals

The latest wave of procedures filed under the Ordinary Legislative Procedure (COD) marks an acceleration of Commission proposals reaching Parliament. Procedures 2025/0221(COD) through 2025/0241(COD) — more than a dozen new COD files — have been registered and assigned to committees. These sit alongside procedures such as 2025/0207(COD) and earlier filings like 2025/0172(COD) and 2025/0176(COD), all currently at committee stage.

Budget procedures have also expanded, with 2025/0198(BUD), 2025/0206(BUD), and 2025/0210(BUD) now active — reflecting ongoing work on the 2026 EU budget and supplementary financing instruments. Several non-legislative procedures (NLE) have been filed as well, including 2025/0192(NLE), 2025/0199(NLE), 2025/0214(NLE), and 2025/0219(NLE), covering international agreements and institutional appointments.

Earlier in the term, key COD procedures included 2025/0012(COD) on digital-age VAT reform, 2025/0039(COD) and 2025/0040(COD) on industrial policy, and 2025/0056(COD) and 2025/0058(COD) on regulatory frameworks. The pipeline also features 2025/0084(COD) and 2025/0090(COD), both at committee stage.

Recently Adopted Texts

The adopted texts feed highlights seven recently updated items — TA-10-2025-0281, TA-10-2025-0289, TA-10-2025-0294, TA-10-2025-0300, TA-10-2025-0306, TA-10-2025-0307, and TA-10-2025-0308 — indicating Parliament is finalising positions on multiple dossiers simultaneously.

Among the most significant texts adopted during the current term: Parliament approved the Waste Framework Directive on textiles and food waste (TA-10-2025-0167, adopted 9 September 2025), amendments to corporate sustainability reporting and due diligence requirements (TA-10-2025-0064, adopted 3 April 2025, and TA-10-2025-0264, adopted 13 November 2025), and the Common Data Platform on Chemicals (TA-10-2025-0045, adopted 1 April 2025). The re-attribution of scientific tasks to the European Chemicals Agency (TA-10-2025-0236 and TA-10-2025-0237, adopted 21 October 2025) further reinforced Parliament's chemicals regulation agenda.

On trade and economic policy, Parliament approved modified customs duties on imports from Russia and Belarus (TA-10-2025-0109, adopted 22 May 2025), the InvestEU efficiency reforms (TA-10-2025-0296, adopted 26 November 2025), and the EU budget for 2026 (TA-10-2025-0244, adopted 22 October 2025). The mobilisation of the European Globalisation Adjustment Fund for Northvolt workers in Sweden (TA-10-2025-0325, adopted 16 December 2025) highlighted the social dimension of Europe's green transition.

Legislative Pipeline Overview

With 923 procedures registered in 2025 and 78 legislative acts adopted, the pipeline is operating at full capacity. The 2026 projection suggests 114 legislative acts and 565 roll-call votes — a 46% increase in legislative output compared to the transition year of 2024. The Ordinary Legislative Procedure (COD) dominates the workload, but Budget (BUD) and Non-Legislative (NLE) procedures are also significant contributors.

Strategic Context

The legislative agenda of the 10th Parliament reflects a clear shift in priorities. With EPP holding 185 seats (25.7%), S&D at 135 (18.8%), PfE at 84 (11.7%), and ECR at 79 (11%), no two-party coalition commands a majority among the 720 MEPs. This means every major piece of legislation requires agreement across at least three political groups — increasing negotiation complexity but also broadening democratic legitimacy.

Key thematic pillars emerging from the pipeline include: industrial competitiveness (biotechnology strategy, InvestEU reforms, tax simplification), defence and security (reflected in the Common Security and Defence Policy annual report, TA-10-2025-0058), environmental regulation (chemicals, waste, sustainability reporting), and enlargement (reports on Serbia, Türkiye, Kosovo, Albania, and institutional consequences of EU enlargement, TA-10-2025-0247). The European Citizens' Initiative on safe and accessible abortion (TA-10-2025-0338, adopted 17 December 2025) demonstrated Parliament's engagement with citizen-driven legislation.

Stakeholder Impact

Industry stakeholders face significant regulatory changes through the chemicals data platform and sustainability reporting amendments. The postponement of certain CSRD and CSDDD deadlines (TA-10-2025-0064) offers temporary relief to businesses, while the re-attribution of tasks to the European Chemicals Agency reshapes the regulatory landscape for the chemical sector. Workers displaced by Europe's green industrial transition benefit from the EGF mobilisation for former Northvolt employees. EU citizens across all member states gain from strengthened electoral rights (TA-10-2025-0120) and greater transparency in subsidiarity and proportionality processes (TA-10-2025-0309).

Why This Matters

The scale and pace of legislative activity in early 2026 confirms that the European Parliament's 10th term has moved beyond its initial organisational phase into substantive lawmaking. With over 90 new procedures registered and a projected 46% increase in legislative output, the Parliament is addressing the EU's most pressing challenges — from trade defence to industrial strategy to climate regulation. For citizens, businesses, and member states, understanding the trajectory of these procedures is essential for anticipating regulatory changes that will shape Europe's economy and society in the years ahead.
