EU Parliament Committee Activity Report: ENVI, ECON, AFET, LIBE, AGRI

Analysis of recent legislative output, effectiveness metrics, and key committee activities

Five of the European Parliament's most consequential committees — ENVI, ECON, AFET, LIBE, and AGRI — are navigating a period of intense legislative activity as the tenth parliamentary term enters its second full year. With 1,773 committee meetings held and 105 legislative acts adopted so far in EP10, the current term is on track to match or exceed the record productivity of EP9, even as political fragmentation reaches historic highs with a parliamentary fragmentation index of 6.59.

The political arithmetic of EP10 continues to shape every committee negotiation. EPP leads with 188 seats (26.1%), followed by S&D at 136 (18.9%), PfE at 84 (11.7%), ECR at 78 (10.8%), and Renew at 77 (10.7%). The top-two group concentration stands at just 44.5% — well below the majority threshold — meaning committee rapporteurs must build coalitions spanning at least three political families. This structural reality has transformed committee work into the Parliament's primary arena for legislative deal-making.

Strategic Context: EP10's Legislative Engine at Full Speed

With 55 plenary sessions held and 514 roll-call votes recorded in the current term, the Parliament's legislative machinery is operating at high capacity. Committee work underpins this output: the 701 active procedures currently in the pipeline require sustained committee engagement across multiple policy domains. Parliamentary questions have reached 4,612 — reflecting an oversight intensity of 6.41 questions per MEP, demonstrating that committees are actively scrutinising the European Commission's work programme.

The Herfindahl-Hirschman concentration index has fallen to 0.1517 — a historic low — confirming that Europe's legislature has evolved from the EPP-S&D duopoly of the early 2000s into a genuinely multi-polar assembly. The minimum winning coalition now requires three groups, and the grand coalition surplus has narrowed to just 5.0 percentage points. This means that even traditional centrist compromises require careful choreography in committee, where amendments are shaped before reaching the plenary floor.

Committee Activity Analysis

ENVI — Environment, Public Health and Food Safety

ENVI maintains one of the Parliament's heaviest legislative portfolios, with workload intensity rated HIGH and approximately 100 active legislative files in early March 2026. The committee's focus has pivoted from the legislative creation phase of the European Green Deal — largely completed during EP9 — to the more technically complex implementation and review phase. Key files include secondary legislation on the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) reporting requirements, revisions to air quality standards under the Zero Pollution Action Plan, and the pharmaceutical legislation package addressing both access to medicines and environmental impacts of drug manufacturing.

The committee's policy impact remains HIGH, reflecting ENVI's central role in shaping the EU's environmental and health regulatory framework. However, the political balance has shifted: EPP and ECR members are pushing for longer implementation timelines and greater flexibility for industry, while Greens/EFA and S&D defend the ambition levels set during EP9. Renew frequently serves as the swing vote, particularly on files where competitiveness and sustainability objectives intersect. The recent plenary adoption of texts T10-0054/2026 through T10-0065/2026 demonstrates that committee-stage compromises are translating into legislative output, though at a moderated pace compared to EP9's peak.

ECON — Economic and Monetary Affairs

ECON's legislative agenda centres on the EU's financial regulatory framework and fiscal governance architecture, with workload intensity rated HIGH and a robust pipeline of active files. The committee is processing the final elements of the Banking Package (CRR III/CRD VI implementation), advancing Capital Markets Union initiatives aimed at deepening European capital markets, and monitoring the application of the Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA) framework. The Draghi Report on European competitiveness has injected fresh urgency into ECON's work on investment frameworks and regulatory simplification.

The committee's effectiveness metrics show HIGH policy impact, with reports consistently advancing through the legislative pipeline. Several ordinary legislative procedures from 2025 — including proposals on payment services modernisation and anti-money laundering supervision — are at advanced committee stage. The coalition dynamics in ECON tend to produce centre-right to centrist outcomes, with EPP and Renew aligning on market-oriented proposals while S&D and The Left negotiate for stronger consumer protection and financial stability safeguards. PfE's growing influence adds a new variable, particularly on deregulation files.

AFET — Foreign Affairs

AFET operates in a distinctly different legislative mode from other committees, with its agenda dominated by non-legislative procedures — resolutions, recommendations, and consent procedures for international agreements. The committee's workload intensity is HIGH, but its policy impact rating reflects the non-binding nature of much foreign policy work in the Parliament. AFET is currently processing files related to the European Defence Industrial Strategy (EDIS), the EU's enlargement policy framework, and a series of international partnership agreements requiring parliamentary consent.

The geopolitical context of 2026 has galvanised unusual cross-party coalitions in AFET. EPP, ECR, and elements of PfE have found common ground on increased defence spending and strategic autonomy, while Greens/EFA and The Left maintain critical positions on arms exports and militarisation of EU policy. The committee's handling of the evolving European security architecture — including the proposed European Defence Fund expansion and cooperation frameworks with NATO — represents one of the most politically dynamic areas of EP10's work. AFET's resolutions, while non-binding, set the political direction that the Commission and Council increasingly reference in interinstitutional negotiations.

LIBE — Civil Liberties, Justice and Home Affairs

LIBE operates at the intersection of security policy and fundamental rights — a tension that has intensified under EP10's rightward-shifted political composition. The committee's workload intensity is HIGH and its policy impact rating reflects its role in shaping legislation that directly affects citizens' daily lives. Key files include the implementation acts for the Pact on Migration and Asylum, new proposals on border surveillance technology, and the ongoing legislative framework for artificial intelligence in law enforcement.

The coalition dynamics in LIBE are among the most fluid in the Parliament. On migration files, EPP, ECR, and PfE form a bloc favouring stricter enforcement measures, while S&D, Greens/EFA, The Left, and parts of Renew advocate for rights-based approaches. On digital rights and AI regulation, the alignments shift entirely, with centre-left and liberal groups united on data protection while right-leaning groups push for security exemptions. Recent adopted texts updated in the parliamentary record — including resolutions on rule of law monitoring and digital identity frameworks — illustrate LIBE's breadth. The committee's ability to find workable compromises on these politically charged dossiers is a key indicator of EP10's legislative health.

AGRI — Agriculture and Rural Development

AGRI is grappling with the complex intersection of food security, environmental sustainability, and farmer livelihoods as the Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) enters its mid-term review period. The committee's workload intensity is HIGH, driven by ongoing implementation of the 2023-2027 CAP strategic plans, new legislative proposals on sustainable food systems, and emergency measures addressing market volatility in the agricultural sector. The committee is also processing proposals related to pesticide reduction targets and the revision of food labelling requirements.

The political dynamics in AGRI have shifted markedly in EP10. Farmer protests across Europe in 2024-2025 strengthened the hand of EPP and ECR members who argue that environmental requirements must be balanced against agricultural viability. Greens/EFA and S&D continue to defend the Farm-to-Fork Strategy's ambition levels, but the centre of gravity has moved toward flexibility and competitiveness-oriented approaches. Renew occupies a pivotal swing position, particularly on files where trade policy intersects with agricultural standards. The committee's negotiations on CAP flexibility measures and sustainable protein strategy proposals will set the direction of EU agricultural policy for the remainder of the decade.

Stakeholder Impact

The legislative output flowing from these five committees has far-reaching implications across the EU's 27 member states. Industry stakeholders — particularly in energy, finance, agriculture, and technology — are closely monitoring ENVI and ECON for signals on regulatory burden and transition timelines. The shift toward more business-friendly positions in committee negotiations creates opportunities for deregulatory amendments, though the multi-group coalition requirement means that consumer and environmental protections remain substantive elements of final texts.

Civil society organisations face a more challenging advocacy environment in EP10, particularly on migration (LIBE) and environmental policy (ENVI). However, the fragmented political landscape also creates openings: no single political bloc can dominate committee outcomes, and well-evidenced advocacy can influence swing groups like Renew and moderate EPP members. For farmers and rural communities, AGRI's negotiations on CAP flexibility and environmental conditionality will directly shape income support and compliance obligations. National governments are adapting their Council strategies to the new parliamentary arithmetic, recognising that EP10's committee compromises will increasingly define the scope for trilogue negotiations.

What Happens Next

The March-April 2026 plenary calendar includes votes on several major files that have completed committee stage. ENVI's Green Deal implementation package and ECON's Capital Markets Union proposals are expected to reach the plenary floor in the coming weeks. AFET's annual foreign policy resolutions and LIBE's migration implementation acts are also scheduled for spring consideration. AGRI's mid-term CAP review recommendations will shape the Commission's legislative proposals expected in the second half of 2026.

Key indicators to watch include: ENVI's ability to maintain Green Deal ambition while accommodating centre-right demands for flexibility; ECON's progress on financial market deepening amid geopolitical uncertainty; AFET's influence on the EU's defence spending trajectory; LIBE's management of the politically explosive migration implementation phase; and AGRI's negotiation of the balance between environmental sustainability and farmer competitiveness. With the fragmentation index at 6.59 — the highest in EP history — the performance of EP10's committees in forging workable multi-group compromises will determine whether this Parliament can match its predecessors' legislative productivity while navigating an unprecedentedly complex political landscape.

Why This Matters

The European Parliament's committee system is where EU legislation takes shape before reaching the plenary floor. In EP10, where political fragmentation has reached record levels and no two-party majority has been possible since 2019, committee negotiations carry exceptional weight. The five committees examined here — ENVI, ECON, AFET, LIBE, and AGRI — collectively oversee policy domains that affect every EU citizen, from the air they breathe and the food they eat to their financial security, fundamental rights, and the Union's place in the world. The compromises being negotiated in March 2026 across these committees will define the EU's regulatory framework for years to come, making committee-level scrutiny essential for understanding where European governance is headed.
