EP10 Committee Workload Surges as Political Fragmentation Reshapes Legislative Coalitions

Five key committees — ENVI, ECON, AFET, LIBE, and AGRI — each manage 100 active legislative files under the most fragmented Parliament in EU history, with a record effective-party index of 6.14

Analytical Overview

The 10th European Parliament is entering its second year with an extraordinary legislative burden. All five major committees analysed — Environment (ENVI), Economic Affairs (ECON), Foreign Affairs (AFET), Civil Liberties (LIBE), and Agriculture (AGRI) — are rated at HIGH workload intensity, each managing approximately 100 active legislative files. This comes as the parliamentary fragmentation index has reached a record 6.14 effective parties, meaning that no two-party majority has been possible since 2019 and every major legislative act now requires coalitions spanning at least three, often four, political groups.

Committee meetings have surged 30% compared to the EP6 era (2004–2009), reflecting both the growing complexity of EU legislation and the need for intensive cross-party negotiation. With an estimated 2,500 committee meetings projected for 2026 and 110 legislative acts expected to be adopted, committees are the engine room of European lawmaking — and they are running at full capacity.

ENVI — Environment, Public Health and Food Safety

Workload: HIGH (100 active files)

Key Legislative Priorities

ENVI remains the busiest committee in the European Parliament, a status it has held since the Green Deal became the centrepiece of EU policy under EP9. In EP10, the committee faces the dual challenge of implementing landmark legislation passed in the previous term — including the Nature Restoration Law, the Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive, and the revised Emissions Trading System — while advancing new proposals on circular economy targets and the zero-pollution action plan.

The political dynamics have shifted noticeably rightward in EP10. The EPP (188 seats, 26.1% seat share) has reasserted its influence over environmental policy, pushing for what it calls a "pragmatic" approach to Green Deal implementation. This has created friction with Greens/EFA (53 seats) and S&D (136 seats), who argue that watering down environmental standards would undermine the EU's climate credibility. The Patriots for Europe (PfE, 86 seats) and ECR (78 seats) have found common ground in opposing what they describe as regulatory overreach.

ENVI's legislative pipeline health score stands at 100%, indicating strong momentum — but the political complexity of building majorities on environmental files means that committee votes increasingly produce narrower margins than in EP9.

ECON — Economic and Monetary Affairs

Workload: HIGH (100 active files)

Key Legislative Priorities

ECON is navigating a dense legislative agenda centred on economic governance reform, banking union completion, and the regulatory framework for digital finance. The committee's work is shaped by the macroeconomic context: inflationary pressures have eased across the eurozone, but structural challenges — including high sovereign debt ratios in several member states and a widening productivity gap with the United States and China — demand legislative responses.

The digital euro proposal remains one of ECON's most politically sensitive files. The ECB's digital currency project faces scepticism from ECR and PfE members who question the need for a central bank digital currency, while Renew Europe (77 seats) has championed the initiative as essential for European monetary sovereignty. The committee must also advance anti-money laundering reforms and the implementation framework for the Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA).

Roll-call vote data shows that ECON files typically achieve adoption through an EPP–S&D–Renew coalition, but this three-party alignment has become less automatic under EP10's more fragmented conditions. With 520 roll-call votes recorded across the Parliament in 2025, ECON accounts for a significant share of the most contested divisions.

AFET — Foreign Affairs

Workload: HIGH (100 active files)

Key Legislative Priorities

AFET operates in a geopolitical landscape that has fundamentally transformed since Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine in 2022. The committee's agenda for 2026 centres on three pillars: continued support for Ukraine's reconstruction and EU accession process, the recalibration of EU-China relations amid growing technological competition, and the development of a credible European defence capability through the SEDE subcommittee.

The enlargement portfolio has gained urgency with Ukraine, Moldova, and several Western Balkan candidates at various stages of the accession process. AFET must balance the political imperative of "keeping the door open" with the institutional challenge of managing EU governance structures designed for fewer member states. The committee's workload reflects this — 100 active files span from sanctions regimes and human rights resolutions to neighbourhood policy and development cooperation.

Cross-party consensus on Ukraine support has remained relatively strong, with EPP, S&D, Renew, and Greens/EFA broadly aligned. However, PfE and ESN (25 seats) have been more critical of aid packages, creating a new fault line in foreign policy debates that did not exist under EP9.

LIBE — Civil Liberties, Justice and Home Affairs

Workload: HIGH (100 active files)

Key Legislative Priorities

LIBE faces what may be the most politically charged workload of any committee in EP10. The implementation of the New Pact on Migration and Asylum — adopted in the final months of EP9 — requires detailed secondary legislation on border procedures, solidarity mechanisms, and reception standards. Migration policy has become the defining political fault line of EP10, with the rightward shift in parliamentary composition producing significantly different voting dynamics than the previous term.

The AI Act enforcement framework is another major LIBE file. The committee is responsible for oversight of the AI Office and for ensuring that high-risk AI systems — particularly those used in law enforcement, border management, and judicial proceedings — comply with fundamental rights safeguards. The tension between security-oriented groups (EPP, ECR, PfE) and civil liberties advocates (S&D, Greens/EFA, The Left with 46 seats) has produced some of the most divisive committee votes of the term.

Rule-of-law monitoring, including the annual Article 7 procedures and the EU Justice Scoreboard, continues to consume significant committee time, with proceedings concerning Hungary and Poland at different stages of political resolution.

AGRI — Agriculture and Rural Development

Workload: HIGH (100 active files)

Key Legislative Priorities

AGRI is managing the ongoing implementation of the reformed Common Agricultural Policy (CAP), which entered its current programming period in 2023. The committee's work in 2026 focuses on mid-term reviews of national Strategic Plans, the adjustment of conditionality requirements following the 2024 farmer protests that shook several member states, and the advancement of the Sustainable Food Systems legislative framework.

The political dynamics in AGRI have shifted markedly. EPP, which has traditionally dominated agricultural policy, has found new allies in ECR and PfE in resisting what they characterise as excessive environmental conditionality in farming regulations. This informal alliance has been able to outvote the S&D–Greens/EFA–Renew coalition that drove the Farm to Fork Strategy under EP9, resulting in significant modifications to pesticide reduction targets and organic farming mandates.

Trade policy intersects heavily with AGRI's work, particularly regarding the Mercosur agreement and the impact of Ukrainian agricultural imports on EU markets. The committee has issued multiple opinions to INTA (International Trade) reflecting member state sensitivities on market access and food standards equivalence.

Strategic Context: The Fragmentation Factor

The overarching story across all five committees is the impact of EP10's unprecedented political fragmentation. With an effective-party index of 6.14 — up from 4.12 in 2004 — the traditional EPP–S&D "grand coalition" that reliably delivered majorities from EP5 through EP8 is no longer sufficient. Current seat distribution (EPP 188, S&D 136, PfE 86, ECR 78, Renew 77, Greens/EFA 53, The Left 46, NI 31, ESN 25) means that legislation requires at least three groups to reach a working majority of 361 out of 720 seats.

This fragmentation is most visible at the committee level, where rapporteurs must negotiate across wider political spectrums to secure committee adoption. Data from 2025 shows that committee meeting frequency — at 2,350 meetings — has continued the upward trend that began with the Lisbon Treaty's expansion of parliamentary competences, reflecting the additional negotiation time required to build viable coalitions.

The legislative pipeline remains healthy, with a pipeline health score of 100% and strong momentum. However, the quality of that pipeline is changing: more files are being adopted with narrower majorities, and the political composition of winning coalitions shifts more frequently depending on policy domain. Environmental and social files tend to attract EPP–S&D–Renew–Greens coalitions, while migration, security, and agricultural files increasingly see EPP–ECR–PfE alignments.

Stakeholder Impact

Industry and business benefit from ECON's continued focus on completing the Capital Markets Union and digital finance regulation, though the slower pace of consensus-building means regulatory timelines may slip. The Green Deal implementation through ENVI creates both compliance costs and investment opportunities for clean-technology sectors.

Civil society organisations monitoring fundamental rights face a more challenging environment in LIBE, where the rightward shift has altered the balance between security measures and civil liberties protections. The AI Act's enforcement framework will be a critical test case for whether the EU can maintain its "human-centric AI" approach under new political conditions.

Farmers and rural communities are seeing AGRI respond more directly to their concerns about regulatory burden, but environmental groups warn that the relaxation of conditionality requirements risks undermining the EU's climate and biodiversity commitments.

EU candidate countries and their citizens are most directly affected by AFET's work on enlargement, where the committee's ability to maintain cross-party consensus on accession processes will determine the pace of EU expansion.

What Happens Next

The spring 2026 committee calendar is expected to be one of the most intensive in EU history. With 110 legislative acts projected for adoption this year (up from 95 in 2025), committees face compressed timelines and complex inter-institutional negotiations. Key milestones to watch include:

  • ENVI: Committee vote on the revised Circular Economy Package, expected in Q2 2026
  • ECON: Trilogue negotiations on the digital euro regulation, ongoing through spring 2026
  • AFET: Assessment of Ukraine's accession progress, with a committee opinion expected by June 2026
  • LIBE: First implementation reports on the Migration and Asylum Pact, due by mid-2026
  • AGRI: Mid-term review of CAP Strategic Plans, with committee hearings scheduled for April–May 2026

With an estimated 580 roll-call votes projected for 2026 and committee meetings expected to reach 2,500, the legislative machinery of the European Parliament continues to accelerate — even as the political consensus required to drive it becomes harder to assemble.

Why This Matters

European Parliament committees are where EU legislation takes shape before reaching the plenary floor. The combination of record workloads and unprecedented political fragmentation in EP10 is fundamentally changing how European laws are made. Narrower committee majorities and shifting coalition patterns mean that legislative outcomes are less predictable and more reflective of issue-by-issue negotiations rather than stable bloc politics. For citizens, businesses, and governments across 27 member states, this evolution in parliamentary dynamics will determine the pace and direction of European integration for years to come.
