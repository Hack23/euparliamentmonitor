Parliamentary Motions & Votes: 2026-03-04

Recent parliamentary motions, voting records, party cohesion analysis, and detected voting anomalies in the European Parliament

The European Parliament's 10th legislative term is charting new territory in early 2026, with unprecedented political fragmentation reshaping how motions and resolutions move through the chamber. With a fragmentation index of 6.59 — the highest in parliamentary history — no two-party grand coalition can command a majority, forcing complex multi-group negotiations on every significant motion. As of March 2026, Parliament has processed 54 adopted texts, 25 resolutions, and conducted 77 roll-call votes across 8 plenary sessions, reflecting the intense legislative pace of EP10's second year.

The adopted texts feed from the European Parliament reveals 15 recently updated texts for 2026 alone, spanning references T10-0054/2026 through T10-0002/2026. These texts cover legislative resolutions, positions on Commission proposals, and non-legislative resolutions addressing urgent geopolitical and domestic policy concerns. The volume signals an accelerating legislative agenda as the Parliament moves beyond its initial organisational phase into full legislative output.

Political Landscape & Group Dynamics

EP10 presents the most fragmented European Parliament since direct elections began in 1979. The EPP remains the largest group with 188 seats (26.1%), followed by S&D with 136 seats (18.9%). Together, these two traditional pillars of EU governance command just 45.0% of seats — well below the simple majority threshold, making the historic grand coalition model unviable.

The right flank of the Parliament has consolidated significantly: Patriots for Europe (PfE) holds 86 seats (11.9%), European Conservatives and Reformists (ECR) controls 78 seats (10.8%), and Europe of Sovereign Nations (ESN) has 25 seats (3.5%). Combined, these three groups represent over 26% of the chamber, creating a substantial right-wing bloc that can block or significantly amend motions.

Renew Europe, the liberal centre, holds 77 seats (10.7%), making it the critical swing force. Any majority coalition must include at least three groups, and most viable combinations require Renew's participation alongside EPP and either S&D or ECR — a dynamic that gives the liberal group outsized influence on motion outcomes.

On the left, Greens/EFA with 53 seats (7.4%) and The Left with 46 seats (6.4%) have seen their combined influence diminished compared to EP9, though they remain pivotal on environmental and social policy motions where centre-right groups are divided.

Party Cohesion & Voting Discipline

With 77 roll-call votes conducted so far in 2026, the early patterns of EP10 voting discipline are taking shape. Historical data shows a legislative output per session of approximately 1.88 acts, with a roll-call vote yield of 10.27 — meaning each plenary session produces roughly 10 recorded votes on motions and amendments.

The minimum winning coalition size of 3 groups means that party discipline within each group is more critical than ever. A single group's defection can collapse a carefully negotiated majority, particularly on contentious motions related to migration, defence spending, or Green Deal implementation. The Herfindahl-Hirschman Index has fallen to 0.1517, confirming deconcentration from a near-duopoly to a multi-polar party system where no group can dictate outcomes alone.

MEP oversight intensity has risen to 8.54 questions per MEP, up from 5.76 in 2004, indicating structurally stronger Commission scrutiny. This heightened questioning activity suggests that motions for resolution are increasingly being used as tools to pressure the Commission on implementation of existing legislation.

Parliamentary Questions & Oversight

The 837 parliamentary questions filed in 2026 represent a significant oversight workload. With 720 active MEPs, this translates to an average of 1.16 questions per MEP — a rate that, if sustained, would exceed the EP9 average and reflect growing use of written and oral questions as political positioning tools.

Priority questions and interpellations have become particularly important in EP10, where the fragmented political landscape means that formal legislative channels are slower. MEPs increasingly use questions to flag concerns about Commission inaction, Member State non-compliance, and emerging policy gaps — particularly in areas like AI regulation enforcement, defence procurement coordination, and energy security.

The 322 committee meetings held in 2026 provide the infrastructure for this oversight activity. Committee-level scrutiny generates many of the parliamentary questions that subsequently inform motions for resolution, creating a pipeline from technical examination to political action.

Strategic Context

The 2026 motions landscape must be understood against three structural forces shaping EP10. First, the rightward shift in parliamentary composition has redrawn coalition mathematics on security, migration, and industrial policy motions. Second, the fragmentation index of 6.59 — up from 4.12 in 2004 — means that building majorities requires unprecedented cross-party negotiation. Third, the geopolitical environment, including ongoing security concerns in Europe's neighbourhood, has elevated defence and foreign affairs motions to a prominence not seen since the early 2000s.

The top-2 group concentration of 45.0% has crossed below the critical 50% threshold since 2019, representing a structural regime change. Every legislative majority now requires three or more groups, increasing negotiation complexity and the potential for motion amendments that water down or redirect original proposals.

With 1740 speeches delivered across 8 plenary sessions in 2026, the debate intensity per session remains high at approximately 217.5 speeches per session, indicating robust engagement on motions across the political spectrum.

Why This Matters

The motions and resolutions passing through the European Parliament in early 2026 reveal a legislature in transformation. The unprecedented fragmentation of EP10 means that no single political vision dominates — instead, motions succeed only through complex, often ad hoc coalitions that shift depending on the policy domain. For EU citizens, this means legislative outcomes are increasingly the product of compromise between three or more political families, potentially producing more balanced but slower policy responses.

The 15 recently updated adopted texts for 2026 signal that despite structural fragmentation, the Parliament remains productive. The key question for the months ahead is whether this multi-polar dynamic will facilitate or impede action on the major challenges facing Europe: defence integration, AI governance, climate transition, and migration management. The motions tabled and adopted in this early phase of EP10 will set the political parameters for the remainder of the term.
